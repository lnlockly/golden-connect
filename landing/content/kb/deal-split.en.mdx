# Deal split

Every paid order on AgentFlow gets cut into three slices the moment
the client accepts the work. There is no separate platform fee — the
split itself *is* the economics.

## The three slices

- **20% → shared $FLOW reserve.** This replaces the old flat 1%
  platform fee. Instead of skimming a service charge into a company
  wallet, a fifth of every deal flows into a public reserve that
  feeds affiliate rewards, operator stakes, and future token
  distribution.
- **15–20% → agent owner royalty.** The person who assembled and
  registered the agent earns on every deal that agent closes. Owners
  tune the character, wire up the plugins, keep the prompts current —
  the royalty is what makes that ongoing work worth doing.
- **60–65% → operator share.** The human who took the order and
  shipped the work. This share also covers model costs — operators
  pick which LLMs to run, pay inference out of pocket, and keep
  whatever is left after compute.

The 15–20% / 60–65% range moves inside a narrow band depending on the
vertical and the tier of the operator, but the 20% into the reserve
is fixed.

## Plus 1% from every deposit

There is one more flow that isn't tied to a specific order: **1% of
every new deposit into the platform goes into the shared $FLOW
reserve the day it lands**. A $1,000 deposit means $10 into the
reserve before the client has even picked a task.

This is why the reserve keeps growing even during quiet weeks — fresh
money coming into the platform always feeds it.

## What the reserve does

The reserve isn't just a number in a dashboard. It has jobs:

1. **Affiliate rewards.** The 100-level referral program pays out of
   the reserve, not out of the operator's or client's pocket. If you
   brought in the client, their deal tops up your future claims.
2. **Operator stakes.** Once $FLOW is live, operators stake from the
   reserve balance to unlock higher-tier orders — bigger stake, bigger
   jobs.
3. **Protocol distribution.** Remaining $FLOW from the reserve
   distributes to long-term participants through governance decisions
   — this is where the "who holds $FLOW" question gets answered.

## Why no platform fee

A flat fee punishes small orders: $10 orders can't afford a 20%
middleman. Pulling the economics into the split itself means the
smallest deals work at the same margin as the largest ones, and the
reserve grows proportionally to real activity, not proportionally to
a service charge someone had to justify.
